Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used for health information seeking, but alignment techniques encode predominantly Western norms: patient autonomy, direct communication,and biomedical exclusivity. When deployed in Central African contexts, these models fail catastrophically. Lingala, spoken by over 45 million people, lacks any evaluated LLM alignment dataset for health. Women’s cancers (breast, cervical, ovarian) are particularly critical: cervical cancer incidence in DRC is 35.8 per 100,000, yet most women present at late stages due to cultural taboos and miscommunication. We present the first culturally grounded alignment dataset for women’s cancer questions in Lingala (622 examples across 16 categories), and a cross cultural reward modeling method that generalizes across Lingala and French. Our method achieves a Cultural Safety Score of 4.53/5 (49% relative improvement over standard RLHF), a Cross-Cultural Safety Score of 3.46/5, and reduces safety drift to 0.04. We introduce novel metrics including the Culturally Grounded Actionability Score (1.0) and the Culturally Parametric Safety Score (0.93), providing a more comprehensive evaluation framework for cross-cultural LLM alignment.
